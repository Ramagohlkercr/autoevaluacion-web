Could humanoid robots be heading for the battlefield?

Zoe Corbyn

Technology Reporter, San Francisco

18 hours ago

I've come to an industrial space in a tech-heavy area of San Francisco expecting to see a menacing humanoid robot soldier doing something combat-like: the future of land-based warfare, perhaps. Instead, the black shiny faceless Phantom robot is engaged in "free play", manipulating a bunch of coloured kids' blocks.

"We need data from it just interacting with its environment… and this is today's menu," explains Sankaet Pathak, co-founder and CEO of two-year-old start-up Foundation Robotics, which is developing Phantom for military and civilian applications.

While many companies are building autonomous humanoid robots for factories, homes or companions, Foundation claims it is the only US firm developing them specifically for a broad range of defence applications.

Frontline weaponisation

That includes support roles like supply pickup, reconnaissance, recovery of equipment or casualties, and hazard inspection. But also, more controversially, warfighting to engage and neutralise threats – which Pathak calls "frontline weaponisation".

Arming robots could keep human soldiers out of harm's way, he argues. They could enter and search buildings, where chokepoints can be lethal, and could also reduce collateral damage.

"If there was a war in Taiwan today, the likelihood that China is going to militarise these humanoids and fight effectively is fanciful." Dean Fankhauser, Robozaps

A major engineering challenge

Foundation's goal, Pathak adds, is to produce at least 40,000 units a year by the end of 2027, with long-term costs of less than $20,000 (£15,000) each. The company has $24m in research contracts to pilot its technology with the US military, plus two units being tested by the Ukrainian military.

Perhaps the biggest challenge is developing artificial intelligence that can operate in the real world and cope with unpredictable situations. Phantom is directed by an AI system called Cortex, where a "reasoning model" and a broader "world model" work together.

Ethical concerns loom large

Lethal autonomous weapons, whatever their form, lower the barrier to warfare, dehumanise conflict and blur accountability, says Nicole van Rooijen, executive director of Stop Killer Robots. The answer to the current technological arms race, she argues, is international rules to de-escalate it.
